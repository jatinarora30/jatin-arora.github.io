Why robot safety is a runtime problem

Planning-time guarantees are valuable, but real robots fail at execution time due to uncertainty, delayed reactions, perception noise, and dynamic humans. This note sketches a practical way to think about “runtime safety primitives” like stop, safe-mode, and recovery.

1. The gap: plan vs execution

Many systems optimize a plan or policy assuming a state estimate \( \hat{s}_t \). In deployment, the robot experiences uncertainty and delays; the executed action \(a_t\) is applied under dynamics the planner didn’t fully model.

A useful framing is constrained control / CMDP-style:

$$ \max_{\pi}\; \mathbb{E}_{\pi}\left[\sum_{t=0}^{T} r_t\right] \quad \text{s.t.} \quad \mathbb{E}_{\pi}\left[\sum_{t=0}^{T} c_t\right] \le \epsilon. $$

2. Runtime safety primitive

In practice, when the robot cannot confidently guarantee safety, it needs an explicit “safe fallback” behavior. One simple abstraction:

$$ \text{If } \Pr(\text{Harm} \mid \hat{s}_t, a_t) > \delta \text{ or UncertaintyHigh} \;\Rightarrow\; \text{Stop / SafeMode / Retreat}. $$

3. Why this matters for fleets

Fleet deployment adds constraints: latency, degraded sensors, and human unpredictability. These are exactly the regimes where runtime safety dominates.
