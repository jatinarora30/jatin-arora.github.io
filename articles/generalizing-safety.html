Generalizing Robot Safety

The phrase “Safety first” is commonly seen across factories, shop floors, and machine operating areas. As robots increasingly move beyond controlled industrial settings into homes, warehouses, hospitals, and public spaces, ensuring their safe operation becomes a fundamental concern. Failures in safety are not merely inconvenient; in many cases, they can be hazardous or even fatal.

Consider the example of an autonomous vehicle driving at high speed on an open highway. If an animal suddenly enters the road, delayed reaction time or uncertainty in perception can lead to catastrophic outcomes. Such failures rarely arise from a single component alone. Instead, they often emerge from the interaction between perception, decision-making, planning, and control. Despite significant advances in robotic capabilities, safety remains a central and unresolved challenge.

This article poses an open and fundamental question: How can robot safety be generalized across different domains of robotic systems?

Different research communities interpret robot safety in distinct ways. Some representative perspectives are outlined below.

  • Control theory: Safety is typically defined in terms of invariance, where system trajectories are required to remain within a predefined safe set. This notion is commonly enforced using control barrier functions, which restrict admissible control inputs to guarantee safety.
  • Reinforcement learning: Safety is formulated as reward maximization under constraints. Problems are often modeled as Constrained Markov Decision Processes (CMDPs) and addressed using techniques such as Lagrangian methods, risk-sensitive objectives (e.g., CVaR), penalty-based approaches, or barrier-shaped reward functions.
  • Formal methods and programs: Safety is specified using logical formalisms such as Linear Temporal Logic, where desired behaviors and forbidden states are encoded explicitly and verified against system executions.
  • Perception systems: Safety is closely tied to uncertainty. Failures may arise from unobserved regions, blind spots, misclassifications, or dynamically changing environments, all of which can invalidate downstream assumptions made by planners or controllers.
  • Path planning: Safety is often defined geometrically, aiming to compute feasible paths that maintain sufficient clearance from obstacles. Common strategies include obstacle inflation, conservative heuristics, and geometric safety margins.

Each of these domains provides a well-defined notion of safety within its own scope. However, robotic systems are composed of multiple such modules operating simultaneously. A key challenge arises when these independently defined notions of safety must interact within a single system.

This leads to a fundamental question: if safety is guaranteed at the level of individual components, how can we ensure that safety is preserved when these components operate together in a closed-loop robotic system?
